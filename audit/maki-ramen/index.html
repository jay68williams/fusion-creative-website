Where The Opportunity Is
Five Things Holding You Back

The brand, the audience, and the product are all strong. But there are some structural issues and strategic gaps that are limiting your growth. Here is where the biggest opportunities are.

1

0.33% Engagement Rate with 70K Followers Is a Red Flag

With 70,811 followers, the benchmark engagement rate for a food brand is 1-3%. That means you should be getting 700 to 2,100 likes per post. You are averaging 231. The audience exists but is not being activated.

This is not a follower quality problem. The Halal Table series proved the audience will engage when content speaks directly to them. The issue is that most of the content is not triggering saves, shares, and comments. It is being scrolled past. Content needs to be engineered to provoke a response, not just look good.

Every post that underperforms tells the algorithm to show the next post to fewer people. It is a compounding problem. The good news is that the Halal Table already showed you the fix. The audience is there. They just need a reason to stop scrolling.

2

Following 4,591 Accounts Dilutes the Brand

For a national chain with 70K followers, following 4,591 accounts looks messy and undermines authority. Compare this to brands in a similar position: most follow under 500. Some follow under 100.

A high follow count signals that the account was grown through follow-unfollow tactics or that nobody is curating the following list. Neither perception helps a brand that is positioning itself as a premium Japanese food experience. Cleaning this up is a simple fix that immediately changes how the profile looks to new visitors.

3

The Halal Table Approach Should Be Replicated Across Other Communities

You proved that niche content works. 732 likes and 66 comments from a series that spoke directly to the Halal community. That is 3x your average engagement. The question is: why is this not being done for every audience segment?

A vegan series for the plant-based community. A student series for university towns where you have locations. A match day series at branches near stadiums. Each audience segment deserves dedicated content that makes them feel seen. The Halal Table gave you the playbook. Now run it across every community you serve.

4

The Soho Launch Was Underutilised on Content

Opening a location in one of London's most famous food streets should have been a month-long content event. Influencer partnerships, behind-the-scenes build-out footage, opening night energy, first customer reactions, local food blogger collaborations, Soho street culture integration.

Instead, it got one announcement post with 293 likes. For context, Soho is one of the most Instagram-dense postcodes in the UK. The foot traffic alone should generate UGC. A proper launch campaign coordinated with London-based food creators could have driven thousands of new followers and positioned Maki & Ramen as a Soho destination, not just a chain that opened another branch.

Every future location opening is a content opportunity. The next one should be treated as a campaign, not a single post.

5

No TikTok Link in Bio Despite Having Viral-Ready Products

Food is one of the strongest categories on TikTok. Sushi push pops alone could carry an entire TikTok account. The visual novelty, the first-bite reaction, the "wait, what is that?" factor. It is built for TikTok.

With 70K on Instagram and products that are inherently shareable, TikTok should be a priority growth platform. The bio mentions @ikigairamenuk but there is no TikTok link. Cross-promoting between platforms compounds growth. Every Instagram follower who also follows on TikTok sees your content twice as often.

The ramen-making process (8 hours of broth preparation), the Taiko drum welcome, the sushi push pop reveal. All of this is TikTok-native content waiting to be made.

Ready-Made Content
5 Video Ideas You Could Film This Week

These are formats that are working right now for food brands on Instagram and TikTok. All of them play to Maki & Ramen's strengths as a national Japanese food brand with craft, culture, and viral-ready products.

Idea 01
"8 hours for one bowl of ramen"
Process Craft Storytelling

Time-lapse of the broth being made from scratch. The ingredients going in. The hours passing. The final bowl being assembled and served. Show the craft and patience behind what looks like a simple bowl. No talking, just the process, the steam, and the final slurp.

Why it works: Process content is one of the most saved formats on social media. People are fascinated by the time and effort behind food they take for granted. "8 hours" in the caption immediately stops the scroll. It positions Maki & Ramen as craft, not fast food. This type of content regularly hits millions of views for ramen brands globally.
Idea 02
"We asked 100 people to try sushi push pops"
Reaction Viral Street

Street food activation footage from Soho. Reactions from people trying push pops for the first time. The confusion, the first bite, the surprise. Cut together with trending audio. Fast cuts, genuine reactions, end with a "where is this?" text overlay.

Why it works: Reaction content drives massive shares because people tag friends saying "we need to try this." The push pop is visually novel enough to stop the scroll on its own. Filming in Soho adds aspirational location value. This is the kind of content that gets picked up by food pages and aggregators.
Idea 03
"The Halal Table: Season 2"
Series Community Niche

Build on the most successful content series. Go deeper. Visit the suppliers. Show the certification process. Cook with community members. Make it a proper series with episodes. Each episode focuses on a different aspect of what makes Maki & Ramen Halal-friendly.

Why it works: The Halal Table already proved this works with 732 likes and 66 comments. Season 2 builds on proven success rather than guessing. The Halal food community is highly engaged and shares content within their networks. A series format keeps people coming back and builds anticipation for the next episode.
Idea 04
"Every ramen on our menu, ranked"
Ranking Engagement Debate

Staff ranking every ramen from bottom to top. Genuine debate. Controversial picks. "How is the Tonkotsu not number one?!" Content designed to drive comments and disagreement. Film the reactions, the arguments, and let the audience weigh in.

Why it works: Ranking content drives massive comment engagement because everyone has an opinion. "You put the Miso WHERE?!" is exactly the kind of debate the algorithm rewards. It also subtly showcases the full menu to people who might only order one dish. Comments and saves are the two strongest signals for reach.
Idea 05
"POV: You just walked into Maki & Ramen Soho"
POV ASMR Immersive

The Taiko drum welcome. The atmosphere. The menu being placed down. The first ramen arriving. The chopsticks breaking apart. The first slurp. Immersive, no talking, just sound and visuals. Shot in first person. The full sensory experience in 30 seconds.

Why it works: POV and ASMR food content is one of the highest-saving formats on both platforms. The Taiko drum welcome is a unique differentiator that no other ramen chain has. It creates a "I need to experience this" feeling that drives footfall. This format works especially well for Soho where the aspirational location adds another layer.
Digital Enhancements
Beyond Social Media

These are strategic changes that would make a real difference alongside the social media work.

Clean Up the Following List

Unfollow to under 500. A 70K follower brand following 4,591 accounts looks messy and undermines authority. The ratio matters for first impressions.

  • Audit the following list and remove inactive accounts, competitors, and irrelevant follows
  • Keep brand partners, suppliers, key influencers, and location-relevant accounts
  • Target under 500 following for a clean, authoritative profile appearance
  • Do this gradually over 2-3 weeks to avoid triggering platform flags

Quick win: This is a free fix that immediately changes how your profile looks to new visitors. When someone lands on your page and sees 70K followers but 4.5K following, it raises questions. Under 500 tells a different story.

Add TikTok to Bio and Cross-Promote

The bio mentions @ikigairamenuk but there is no TikTok link. Sushi push pops alone could carry a TikTok account. The visual novelty, the reaction potential, the shareability. It is built for the platform.

  • Add TikTok link to Instagram bio using a link-in-bio tool
  • Cross-post top-performing Reels to TikTok with platform-native edits
  • Create TikTok-first content around push pops, ramen builds, and the Taiko drum
  • Food is one of TikTok's strongest categories. 70K on Instagram should translate to rapid TikTok growth

Create Location-Specific Content Calendars

Each branch should have content that feels local, not just reposts from the main account. The MetroCentre audience cares about different things than Soho. Edinburgh regulars have different triggers than Manchester first-timers.

  • Partner with local food bloggers at each location for location-specific UGC
  • Create city-specific content that references local landmarks, events, and culture
  • Use location tags strategically to build local discovery
  • Feature staff at each branch to humanise the local experience

A national brand that feels local at every location is far more powerful than one that broadcasts the same content everywhere.
